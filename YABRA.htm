YABRA: Yet Another Bad RPG Acronym

YABRA:

Yet Another Bad RPG Acronym

Note:  This game is an experiment.  It is an attempt by me to engineer an entire RPG system in a few hours.  It is a very cinematic game, perhaps best suited to action movie type games.  It is short, poorly edited, untested, and probably bad, though I hope not.

 

Characters

Characters in Yabra are described by a set of attributes.  These describe their capabilities within the game world.  They are rated on a scale of 0 to 10.  Each scale is associated with a rough level of skill, as follows:

0:  Unskilled, bad

2:  Amateur

4:  Good enough to make money at it

6:  Professional

8:  Damn good, probably well known

10:  Freakin’ God

 

The Attributes are:

 

Strength:  For lifting stuff, not really all that useful in the long run though.

Agility:  For performing acrobatic and athletic maneuvers, dodging, shooting, hitting

Endurance:  For taking hits, resisting poison, torture, disease

Intelligence:  Covers scientific type junk, and general mental stores of useless knowledge, as well as thinking skills

Perception:  Noticing stuff.

Whup’ Ass:  For causing damage.

 

The last one deserves explanation.  This stat is a general stat for combat effectiveness.  It helps to narrow down the wide range of different ways of whupping ass into just one single attribute.  One character might be a martial artist, another a crack marksman, another an expert weapons tuner, and all have the same level of Whup’ Ass, just different kinds.  It also covers the tendency of action hero types to be equally as effective regardless of the weapon they happen to be using.  Ever notice how the hero seems to take people down as quickly with a gun as he does with his fists?  That’s Whup’ Ass.  One character might be physically weaker that another, but due to martial art skill, or superb marksmanship, or a detailed knowledge of anatomy, is capable of causing more damage than another.

Characters also have another, non-numerical attribute.  Their Profession.  This is a noun of some sort that describes what the character does for a living.  Any time he does something related to that profession, he gets a +2 on his roll.

Creating characters is simple.  Come up with a concept.  Take 35 points, then assign them to the different attributes, as you think fits with your concept.  Then pick an appropriate profession.  Give him a name.  Voila, you have a character.

 

Rolling the Dice

The GM determines a difficulty, based on what skill level would be needed for the task to be an easy one for the character in question.  For example, shooting the side of a barn would be easy for anyone with a skill of 1, while hitting a can off a fence post at 200 yards might be easy for someone with a skill of at least 7 or more.

Then compare it to the PC’s attribute, plus 2 if the action is related to his profession.  If the PC’s level is higher, he automatically succeeds.  If the PC’s skill is lower by an amount of 6 or more, then he automatically fails.  Otherwise, both PC and GM roll a 6-sided die.  The PC adds the roll to his skill, and the GM adds his roll to the difficulty.  The numbers are compared, and if the PC’s is higher, he succeeds.  Otherwise he fails.

 

Opposed Rolls

Opposed rolls are even easier.  Each side rolls a six-sided die, adds it to the appropriate skill, then whoever got the highest number wins.

 

Combat

All players involved roll a six-sided die, and add that to their Intelligence stat.  Whoever gets the highest number goes first, and play order follows with the next highest, and so on.

To attack the attacker makes an opposed Agility roll with his opponent.  If he succeeds he makes another opposed roll between his Whup’ Ass and the opponent’s Endurance.  If he succeeds in doing damage, take the difference between the two rolls, and subtract it from the opponent’s Endurance stat. 

If Endurance reaches 0, the character is unconscious or dead.  The GM should use his discretion here.

 

Experience

After a successful session, give everyone about 1 – 3 points depending on how well they did.  Also, at the end of a session, who ever gave the coolest description of an action, as judged by the GM, and the other players, gets 2 free experience points.  To raise an attribute, it costs a number of points equal to the current level.  Experience points can also be used in game to add a temporary +1 a piece to a character’s skill for the sake of one roll.
